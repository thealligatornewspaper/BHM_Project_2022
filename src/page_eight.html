    Faces of Black Alachua County Leaders

    Giovanna Mompremier, UF Student Body Treasurer, sits in her office inside the Reitz Union on Tuesday, Feb. 22. “Coming from a post-COVID atmosphere has brought back the overall celebration in knowing that we need to still continue to celebrate the strides and milestones that the Black students, faculty and Black community in Gainesville has done.” Mompremier said. “I’m just so happy being in this space where I can encourage others around me to support and celebrate Black History Month as well.
    Cynthia Chestnut, 72, engages with members of the Gainesville community after her swearing-in as city commissioner on Thursday, Feb. 17. Chestnut was previously elected as the first Black, female city commissioner in 1987 and as mayor in 1989. “I promise to always listen to you,” Chestnut said at the ceremony. “I promise to hear the concerns of the people of Gainesville and advocate for them inside the walls of our City Hall.”
    Roberta Conyers, a Gainesville-based artist, poses with her artwork at the Black Arts & Food Market hosted by Student Government’s Change Party outside of the A Rai of Sunshine Thrift Store on Saturday, Feb. 19. Her art often centers around all aspects of Black life. “I just want to inspire others to create and do what they are passionate about,” Conyers said.
    Evelyn Foxx, president of the Alachua County Branch of the NAACP, poses for a portrait in front of Gainesville City Hall on Thursday, Feb. 17. Foxx attended the swearing in ceremony of newly elected City Commissioner Cynthia Chestnut. “Personally, I celebrate Black History Month 12 months of the year because our history is very important.” Foxx said. “I am also very disturbed that Black history is not being taught in Alachua County.
    Courtney Moore Taylor, African American studies lecturer and faculty adviser for Sankofa Society, speaks to members of the Sankofa African American Studies Society on Wednesday, Feb. 23. The club aims to support the African American studies program through alumni networking and professional development. “To showcase Black learning showcases the possibility that we all as human beings have to excel,” Taylor said.
    Tina Certain, Alachua County School Board member, poses for a portrait at her podium at the Alachua County School District on Wednesday, Feb. 15. Certain, who graduated from Gainesville High School and the UF with an accounting degree, is one of two African American women who serve on the School Board. “If you’ve seen someone who’s done it, it sparks in your mind that you can do it,” Certain said.
    David Canton, director of UF’s African American Studies Department, teaches his History of Hip-Hop class in Turlington Hall on Wednesday, Feb. 16. “In my class we study the history of hip-hop music from an African American perspective. We explore the political — deindustrialization, technology and institutional racism — and cultural — call and response, DJ’s and funk and disco music — forces that created a billion dollar industry.”
    Rachel Grant, UF assistant professor of journalism, poses for a portrait in her office at Weimer Hall on Tuesday, Feb. 8. She teaches Media, Cultures and Identity — a required journalism major class that centers around race, gender and diversity and inclusion topics. Grant was the sixth African American to earn her Ph.D from the Missouri School of Journalism. “It’s not typical to see people who look like me in these positions because of some of the challenges we’ve had to overcome due to racism,” Grant said. “I feel as if I am helping shape the minds of future communicators.
